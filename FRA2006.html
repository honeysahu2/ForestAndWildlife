Forest Rights Act, 2006

The Forest Rights Act (FRA) of 2006, also known as the Scheduled Tribes and Other Traditional Forest Dwellers (Recognition of Forest Rights) Act, is a landmark legislation enacted by the Government of India. The primary objective of the act is to recognize and vest the forest rights and occupation in forest land in forest dwelling Scheduled Tribes (STs) and other traditional forest dwellers (OTFDs) who have been residing in forests for generations but whose rights have not been recorded. Here's a detailed explanation of the key provisions of the Forest Rights Act: Recognition of Forest Rights: The FRA recognizes the rights of forest-dwelling Scheduled Tribes (STs) and other traditional forest dwellers (OTFDs) over forest land and resources that they have been traditionally using for generations but whose rights have not been officially recorded. Types of Rights Recognized: Individual Rights: Rights such as ownership, access to collect, use, and dispose of minor forest produce, habitat rights for primitive tribal groups, and rights to use and/or protect customary boundaries of habitat areas. Community Rights: Rights over community forests and resources traditionally accessed by communities. Process of Recognition: Filing Claims: Forest dwellers can file claims for recognition of their rights with the Gram Sabha (village assembly) within the stipulated timeframe. Verification and Decision: Claims are verified and decided upon by the Gram Sabha and subsequently by the Sub-Divisional Level Committee (SDLC) and District Level Committee (DLC) based on prescribed procedures. Role of Gram Sabha: The Gram Sabha plays a crucial role in the implementation of the act by facilitating the process of filing and verification of claims, ensuring transparency, and safeguarding the interests of forest dwellers. Conservation Provisions: While recognizing the rights of forest dwellers, the act also contains provisions for the conservation and management of forests and wildlife, ensuring that the rights are not exercised in a manner detrimental to the conservation objectives. Protection from Eviction: Once the rights are recognized and vested, forest dwellers are protected from eviction or displacement from their traditional habitats. Ongoing Challenges: Despite the enactment of the FRA, there have been challenges in its effective implementation, including delays in the recognition of rights, inadequate awareness among forest dwellers, conflicts over overlapping claims, and resistance from forest bureaucracy and vested interests. Overall, the Forest Rights Act of 2006 represents a significant step towards recognizing and securing the rights of forest-dwelling communities, promoting social justice, and ensuring sustainable forest management in India.
